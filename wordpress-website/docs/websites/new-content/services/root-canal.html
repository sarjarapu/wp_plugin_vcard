Do You Need a Root Canal?

Call us if you notice any of these symptoms—early treatment is faster, more comfortable, and often prevents infection from spreading to nearby teeth or bone.

  • Throbbing tooth pain that lingers after hot, cold, or sugary foods
  • Pressure sensitivity or discomfort when chewing on one side
  • Swollen, tender, or pimple-like gums near a specific tooth
  • Darkening or discoloration of one tooth after trauma or decay
  • Loose-feeling tooth caused by infection weakening bone support

How Root Canal Treatment Works

Here’s exactly what happens when you schedule your root canal in Cedar Park, so you know what to expect before you walk through the door.

1. Precise Diagnosis

We capture CBCT scans or focused digital X-rays to visualize the canals, measure infection, and confirm if the tooth can be preserved. You’ll see the images on-screen and understand every recommendation.

2. Gentle Anesthesia & Access

Topical gel, buffered local anesthetic, and nitrous oxide (if requested) keep you comfortable. We isolate the tooth with a dental dam, create a small access opening, and remove the inflamed pulp.

3. Cleaning & Sealing

Rotary files, ultrasonic irrigation, and antimicrobial rinses disinfect the canals. We fill them with gutta-percha, seal the opening, and place a protective temporary restoration.

4. Restore Strength

Most treated teeth are finished with a custom crown to prevent fractures. We’ll schedule you for a final visit, guiding you through material choices and linking to our dental crowns page if you want more detail.

Root Canal FAQs

Cedar Park patients ask these questions most often, so we’ve answered them in one place.

Will a root canal hurt?

No. Local anesthesia, topical gel, and optional nitrous oxide keep you comfortable. Most people feel less discomfort than they expected and actually leave pain-free because the infection is gone.

How long does the appointment take?

Plan on 60–90 minutes for a single-rooted tooth and up to two hours for molars. Complex cases may be split into two visits so we can medicate the canal before sealing it.

Do I really need a crown afterward?

In most cases, yes. Removing infected pulp hollows the tooth slightly. Capping it—similar to the guidance on our dental crowns page—prevents cracks and keeps it strong for chewing.

What happens if I delay treatment?

The infection can spread to your jawbone, face, or bloodstream and may force an extraction. Root canals stop the infection, preserve your natural tooth, and avoid costlier implants later.

Why not just extract the tooth?

Removing a tooth can solve short-term pain but creates long-term challenges—shifting teeth, bite changes, and the need for bridges or implants. A root canal keeps your natural tooth in place, maintains jawbone support, and is often more affordable than replacing a missing tooth.

How much does a root canal cost?

Fees vary based on the tooth, infection severity, and whether a crown is needed. Most PPO plans cover a portion of treatment, and we offer financing plus an Aviva Wellness Plan for uninsured guests. Schedule a consultation for a personalized estimate—no pricing surprises online.
